About This Project

Liver cirrhosis is a chronic and progressive liver disease that results in irreversible scarring of liver tissue. It can stem from various causes, including chronic alcohol use, hepatitis infections, and non-alcoholic fatty liver disease. If not detected and treated early, it can lead to severe complications such as liver failure and cancer. This project is focused on developing a predictive model to assist in the early detection of liver cirrhosis using advanced machine learning algorithms. Clinical data—such as bilirubin levels, liver enzymes (AST, ALT), albumin, platelet count, and alcohol consumption—are analyzed to assess a patient’s risk. The machine learning models used in this study include Naive Bayes, K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN), Support Vector Machine (SVM), Logistic Regression, and Random Forest. Each model is trained and evaluated on real-world datasets using performance metrics like accuracy, precision, recall, and F1 score to ensure reliability and robustness. By accurately identifying individuals at risk, the project supports early intervention and enables healthcare professionals to tailor treatment strategies to each patient. This not only improves clinical outcomes but also optimizes healthcare resource utilization. Overall, the project demonstrates the transformative potential of machine learning in hepatology, providing a powerful tool for data-driven diagnostics and proactive liver care.

Feature Overview and Normal Ranges

1. Bilirubin

  • A waste product formed during the breakdown of red blood cells. Processed by the liver and excreted in bile.
  • Normal Range: 0.1 – 1.2 mg/dL
  • Clinical Significance: High levels may indicate liver dysfunction, bile duct blockage, or hemolytic anemia.

2. AST (Aspartate Aminotransferase)

  • An enzyme found in the liver, heart, and muscles. Involved in amino acid metabolism.
  • Normal Range: 10 – 40 U/L
  • Clinical Significance: Elevated levels may indicate liver inflammation, muscle injury, or alcoholic liver disease.

3. ALT (Alanine Aminotransferase)

  • A liver-specific enzyme that reflects liver cell damage.
  • Normal Range: 7 – 56 U/L
  • Clinical Significance: High ALT levels suggest hepatitis or fatty liver disease.

4. Albumin

  • A protein made by the liver that maintains blood fluid balance and transports substances.
  • Normal Range: 3.5 – 5.0 g/dL
  • Clinical Significance: Low levels may indicate chronic liver disease, malnutrition, or kidney issues.

5. Hemoglobin

  • An oxygen-carrying protein in red blood cells.
  • Normal Range: 12 – 16 g/dL (female), 13.5 – 17.5 g/dL (male)
  • Clinical Significance: Low levels may indicate anemia or chronic illness; liver diseases can contribute to low hemoglobin.

6. Platelet Count

  • Plays a key role in blood clotting and stopping bleeding.
  • Normal Range: 1.5 – 4.5 lakhs/mm³
  • Clinical Significance: Low count can occur in liver cirrhosis, bone marrow disorders, or immune conditions.

7. Alcohol Quantity

  • Indicates the average daily intake of alcohol, typically measured in "quarters" where 1 quarter = 180 ml of hard liquor
  • Normal Range:: 0 quarters/day
  • Clinical Significance: High daily alcohol intake is a key risk factor for liver cirrhosis. It damages liver cells, promotes inflammation, and accelerates fibrosis.
